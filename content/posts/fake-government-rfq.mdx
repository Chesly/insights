# How to Spot a Fake Government RFQ in South Africa

Winning government work can be a major milestone for any South African business. Unfortunately, scammers know that many entrepreneurs are eager to secure contracts and often exploit this by sending fake Requests for Quotations (RFQs).

Every year, businesses lose money after paying fake registration fees, delivering goods to fraudsters, or sharing sensitive company information with criminals pretending to represent government departments.

Knowing how to identify a fake RFQ could save your business thousands of rands.

## What Is an RFQ?

A Request for Quotation (RFQ) is an official document issued by an organisation requesting suppliers to provide pricing for specific goods or services.

Government departments, municipalities and state-owned entities regularly issue RFQs for lower-value procurement, while larger projects are usually advertised as formal tenders.

## Why Fake RFQs Are Becoming More Common

Fraudsters often impersonate government institutions to:

- Collect fake supplier registration fees.
- Request upfront administration charges.
- Obtain company registration documents.
- Trick businesses into delivering products without payment.
- Steal banking and tax information.

Small businesses and first-time suppliers are the most common targets.

## 7 Warning Signs of a Fake Government RFQ

### 1. The Email Comes from a Free Email Address

Legitimate government departments normally use official domains such as:

- `@gov.za`
- `@treasury.gov.za`
- `@transnet.net`

Be cautious of emails coming from:

- Gmail
- Yahoo
- Outlook
- Hotmail

when claiming to represent a government institution.

---

### 2. You're Asked to Pay Money

Government departments do **not** charge businesses to:

- Receive RFQs.
- Register as suppliers.
- Submit quotations.
- Join supplier databases.

Any request for payment should immediately raise suspicion.

---

### 3. The Contact Details Cannot Be Verified

Always verify telephone numbers and email addresses by visiting the organisation's official website.

Never rely only on the contact information contained in the RFQ.

---

### 4. Poor Grammar and Formatting

Official procurement documents are generally professionally prepared.

Poor spelling, inconsistent formatting and grammatical mistakes may indicate a fraudulent document.

---

### 5. Unrealistic Deadlines

Scammers often create urgency by saying things such as:

- "Respond within two hours."
- "Payment will be processed today."
- "Immediate supplier required."

Legitimate procurement processes usually allow suppliers enough time to prepare quotations.

---

### 6. Banking Details Don't Match

If you're asked to make any payment, verify banking details independently before transferring money.

Never rely solely on banking details provided in an email.

---

### 7. Something Doesn't Feel Right

Trust your instincts.

If anything appears unusual, contact the organisation directly using contact information published on its official website.

## How to Verify a Government RFQ

Before preparing your quotation, always:

- Confirm the government department exists.
- Verify the procurement official.
- Check the sender's email domain.
- Phone the organisation using publicly listed contact details.
- Confirm whether the opportunity appears on the National eTender Portal where applicable.

Spending a few minutes verifying an RFQ can prevent costly mistakes.

## What Should You Do If You Receive a Fake RFQ?

If you suspect an RFQ is fraudulent:

- Do not make any payments.
- Do not submit confidential company documents.
- Contact the organisation being impersonated.
- Report the incident to SAPS if money has been lost.
- Warn other businesses within your network.

## Common Mistakes Businesses Make

Many businesses become victims because they:

- Assume every government-looking email is genuine.
- Fail to verify contact details.
- Rush to meet unrealistic deadlines.
- Pay "registration fees" that do not exist.
- Deliver products before receiving official confirmation.

Taking a few extra minutes to verify documents can protect your business.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does government charge suppliers to submit quotations?

No. Legitimate government departments do not charge businesses to submit quotations or participate in procurement opportunities.

### Can I receive RFQs without registering on CSD?

Some organisations may invite quotations directly, but registering on the Central Supplier Database significantly improves your chances of being considered for government procurement opportunities.

### What should I do if I've already responded to a fake RFQ?

Immediately contact the organisation being impersonated, report the incident to SAPS if necessary, and monitor your business accounts for any suspicious activity.
